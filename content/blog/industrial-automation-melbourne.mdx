Industrial automation projects in Melbourne are usually justified by a plant issue, not by a technology wish list. The best starting point is the operating constraint itself, whether that is downtime, changeovers, traceability, labour load, or limited visibility.

<div className="blog-note">
  <p>In practice, that means scoping from the installed plant, not only from the drawing set.</p>
</div>

## What matters early

<div className="blog-grid">
  <div className="blog-card">
    <h3>Know the bottleneck</h3>
    <p>If the issue is downtime, do not scope the job around dashboards. If it is traceability, do not scope it around only PLC code.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="blog-card">
    <h3>Plan the full stack</h3>
    <p>Panels, PLCs, SCADA, networks, and commissioning all interact. Treating them as separate jobs creates gaps.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="blog-card">
    <h3>Check the real site</h3>
    <p>On brownfield plants, the installed state often differs from the drawings. That needs to be verified early.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## What to confirm before budget sign-off

<div className="blog-panel">
  <h3>Before the budget is final</h3>
  <ul className="blog-checklist">
    <li>The production problem the project is solving.</li>
    <li>The real I/O, network, and interface picture on site.</li>
    <li>The commissioning model, including FAT, cutover, and rollback rules.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

## What tends to add cost and delay

Most delays come from three familiar issues. Weak discovery. Fragmented scope. Late commissioning planning.

All three are fixable, but they need to be treated as engineering work rather than admin work.

## What this means

If the scope stays tied to a clear plant problem and the installed state is checked early, the project is usually easier to deliver and easier to defend internally.
