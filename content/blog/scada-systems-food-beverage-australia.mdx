SCADA systems in Australian food and beverage manufacturing do far more than display process values on a screen. They are the operational interface through which shift teams manage production, respond to faults, monitor compliance-critical parameters, and record the data that supports traceability and regulatory obligations.

Choosing and implementing a SCADA system is one of the highest-consequence technology decisions a food plant can make. Done well, it improves operator response time, reduces unplanned downtime, supports food safety compliance, and generates production data that drives continuous improvement. Done poorly, it creates an unreliable, cluttered interface that operators learn to work around.

This guide covers SCADA platform selection for Australian food and beverage operations, the engineering principles that produce effective implementations, and the common mistakes that create operational problems after commissioning.

## What SCADA systems need to do in food and beverage manufacturing

Food and beverage SCADA requirements differ from general manufacturing in several important ways. Understanding these requirements before selecting a platform and designing the system prevents costly rework.

**Process visibility across heterogeneous assets.** Food plants operate batching systems, CIP circuits, packaging lines, utilities, and cold storage simultaneously. SCADA must present process state clearly across all these areas without operators having to navigate excessive screen hierarchies.

**HACCP-critical parameter monitoring.** Critical control points such as pasteurisation temperature hold times, metal detector confirmation, and allergen CIP verification must be logged, alarmed, and traceable. SCADA is the primary system that captures this evidence.

**Batch and recipe traceability.** For sites with ISA-88 batch control, SCADA needs to display active batch status, provide recipe parameter access, and record batch events for genealogy reporting.

**GMP-compliant audit trails.** In dairy, ready meals, and other regulated categories, electronic records of setpoint changes, manual overrides, and alarm acknowledgements may need to meet GMP audit trail requirements. This is a design requirement, not a post-implementation add-on.

**Alarm management aligned with ISA-18.2.** Poor alarm management is one of the most persistent problems in food plant SCADA systems. High nuisance alarm rates reduce operator confidence and increase response time to genuine faults.

## SCADA platform options for Australian food plants

Three platforms dominate the Australian food and beverage SCADA market.

### Ignition by Inductive Automation

Ignition has grown rapidly in Australian food and beverage applications over the past decade. Its server-based architecture, unlimited tag licensing model, and Perspective module for web-based operator access make it attractive for sites with multiple production areas or multi-site reporting requirements.

It integrates natively with Rockwell PLC estates via the Logix driver and with Siemens via OPC UA. The Batch module provides ISA-88 batch control capabilities without a separate MES product. The Reporting module supports scheduled production and quality reports.

Key consideration: Ignition implementations are highly configurable, which means implementation quality varies significantly. A poorly structured Ignition deployment can be as difficult to maintain as a poorly structured legacy SCADA, despite the modern architecture.

### Rockwell FactoryTalk View SE

FactoryTalk View SE is the dominant SCADA platform on Rockwell-heavy sites in Australian food and beverage manufacturing. Its deep integration with ControlLogix PLCs, FactoryTalk Historian, and PlantPAx process library gives it a strong advantage on Rockwell estates.

The platform is well understood by local automation engineers and support teams. Its display development tools and alarm server configuration are mature. Its licensing model has historically been tag-based, which increases cost on large deployments.

Key consideration: FactoryTalk View SE requires Windows-based server infrastructure and has specific version compatibility requirements. Server and client PC lifecycle management is an ongoing operational consideration.

### AVEVA System Platform (formerly Wonderware)

AVEVA System Platform is common in larger Australian dairy operations and some multi-site FMCG manufacturers. Its object-based architecture scales well on complex sites with shared process templates. The InTouch HMI component has a long installed base in Australian manufacturing.

Key consideration: AVEVA implementations are typically complex and require experienced platform engineers. They tend to suit organisations with dedicated OT infrastructure teams rather than smaller sites where simplicity and maintainability are higher priorities.

## Screen design principles for food and beverage operators

Screen design quality has a direct effect on operator performance. ISA-101 provides the authoritative framework for HMI design, but its principles can be summarised practically for food and beverage operations.

**Use alarm state colour for alarm indication only.** Red, yellow, and flashing elements should indicate process abnormality. Using these colours decoratively or for navigation reduces the urgency response when genuine alarms occur.

**Show process context, not just values.** A temperature reading without the associated vessel state, CIP phase, or product type requires the operator to apply context from memory. SCADA displays should show enough process state that the current situation is unambiguous.

**Minimise navigation depth for high-frequency tasks.** Operators monitoring a running plant should reach any critical display in two clicks or fewer. Deeply nested screen hierarchies increase response time and error rate during high-pressure events.

**Design faceplates consistently.** Motor faceplates, valve faceplates, and PID faceplates should follow consistent layout and interaction patterns across all production areas. Inconsistency increases training time and error rate, particularly for staff rotating between lines or areas.

**Keep batch and recipe displays close to process displays.** When operators need to verify batch parameters or recipe setpoints during production, those values should be reachable without leaving the process overview.

## Alarm management in food and beverage SCADA

Alarm management is the area where most food plant SCADA systems fail to perform. The problem is almost always that alarm design was treated as an afterthought, with alarms added reactively rather than designed as part of the control philosophy.

ISA-18.2 provides the internationally accepted framework for alarm management. Its core principles translate into specific design requirements.

**Alarm rationalisation.** Every alarm should be associated with a defined operator action and a defined response time. If there is no required action, the point should not be an alarm. Common rationalisation exercises remove 30 to 60 percent of existing alarm points from active alarm lists, which immediately improves the signal-to-noise ratio for operators.

**Alarm priority alignment.** Priority should reflect urgency and consequence, not process importance. Priority 1 alarms should require immediate operator response. Priority 3 alarms can be addressed in the next maintenance window. Most sites have too many Priority 1 alarms, which erodes the concept entirely.

**Suppression and shelving governance.** Food plants frequently suppress alarms during CIP or startup phases to avoid nuisance. Without proper shelving and timeout controls, suppressed alarms can remain inactive indefinitely. SCADA configurations should include automatic re-enable conditions for all suppressed alarms.

**Alarm flood management.** Standing alarms and flood events should be tracked and reviewed in weekly performance meetings. SCADA historians that record alarm and event data make this analysis straightforward and support root cause investigation.

## Historian and production data in food plants

A SCADA system without a historian is a missed opportunity. In Australian food and beverage operations, historian data supports downtime analysis, CIP cycle verification, quality investigations, and batch genealogy.

Key historian design decisions for food plants:

**Tag selection and naming conventions.** Historians capture too much raw data on most sites. Focusing collection on production-critical signals, CCP measurements, utility consumption, and batch events produces a more useful and manageable dataset than blanket collection of every available tag.

**Sampling rates matched to process dynamics.** Temperature trends in a pasteuriser need more frequent sampling than weekly utilities totals. Applying a single sampling rate to all historian tags wastes storage and creates noise in trend displays.

**Batch event and genealogy context.** Where batch control is in use, historian records should include batch identifier, recipe version, and phase events alongside process measurements. This allows traceability queries to retrieve the full production history for a specific lot.

**Integration with reporting.** Production reports that combine historian data with quality and downtime information reduce manual reporting effort and improve data consistency. Ignition Reporting Module, Power BI connected to SQL Historian, and FactoryTalk Analytics are all used effectively in Australian food plants.

## Common mistakes in food plant SCADA projects

These issues appear consistently in SCADA implementations that underperform after commissioning.

**No alarm rationalisation before design.** Building a SCADA system on an unrationalised alarm set transfers the legacy alarm management problem into the new platform. Rationalisation should precede SCADA design.

**Screen design driven by process engineer preference rather than operator research.** Process engineers design SCADA for how they want to see the plant. Operators use SCADA under time pressure to manage production. These are different requirements. Site workshops with shift operators before design begin consistently improve the outcome.

**Historian configuration deferred.** Historian setup is treated as simple and left to commissioning. In practice, historian configuration, tag management, and integration with reporting require significant engineering effort and should be part of the SCADA design scope.

**Network infrastructure not upgraded alongside SCADA.** A new SCADA server connected to an aging, poorly documented OT network inherits all of the network's reliability and security problems. SCADA and OT network projects should be planned together.

**Training limited to system administration rather than operations.** End-user training for shift operators, team leaders, and maintenance technicians is different from system administrator training. Both are required. Operator training in particular should use realistic scenarios from normal production and abnormal event conditions.

## Practical steps for planning a SCADA project

1. Define operational requirements with input from shift operators, supervisors, quality, and maintenance before selecting a platform.
2. Perform alarm rationalisation on the existing system before beginning SCADA design. Do not transfer alarm problems from the old system to the new one.
3. Assess OT network infrastructure and address gaps before SCADA deployment, not after.
4. Develop a screen design standard aligned with ISA-101 principles before building any displays. Retrofit standardisation is expensive.
5. Include historian and reporting requirements in the initial SCADA scope. Treat them as integral, not optional.
6. Budget for operator and maintenance training as a separate line item with adequate time allocation after commissioning.
7. Define SCADA system maintenance ownership before go-live. Identify who is responsible for server updates, backup management, and licence renewals.

A well-designed SCADA system gives food and beverage operations teams the visibility, alarm management, and data they need to improve production performance every shift. Achieving that outcome requires treating SCADA design as an operational engineering discipline rather than a software deployment task.
